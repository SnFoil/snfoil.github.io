Model Rocket Engine Mount

A custom 3D-printed rocket mount for holding E and F class motors for model rockets.

Summary / Table of Contents

This project is a little smaller-scale than my other ones, but I believe I still learned some valuable things from it.

Throughout the development and production of my rocket mounts, I learned more about CAD modeling, 3D printing, and iterative design.

Table of Contents

  • CAD Design
  • 3D Printing & Iterative Design
  • Final Thoughts

CAD Design

For this project, I used Fusion 360. It has a free student license, which is fantastic for me, and I was slightly more familiar with it than other CAD programs such as SOLIDWORKS or AutoCAD.

My only direction on this project was that I wanted something to hold a model rocket engine. I didn't know what it would look like or what size it would be, so I just started.

First, I did some research on the engine I would be using. For this design, I decided I would be using an Estes F15-4 engine. If you don't know what that means, just know that the "F" corresponds to a size. All of the motor's dimensions were listed on Estes' website.

From there, I extended the mount to the sides and up, adding M3 sized screw holes in order to later secure it to a cardboard tube(the rocket's body).

3D Printing & Iterative Design

I initially printed the rocket mount when I thought it was done. However, once I printed the first version, I quickly came to the realization that this thing was not the best it could be. So I went back in to Fusion 360 and began tweaking it. I changed the screw holes to a slightly larger M4, increased the thickness of the vertical engine holders, and gave it another go.

In this stage, I also made another model that would hold a slightly smaller E12-4 engine. The only difference was the positioning of the vertical prongs in the middle.

Through a few more prints and changes in the CAD model, I got to a product that I felt was adequate for the task it needed to perform.

Final Thoughts

Throughtout this project, as touched on in the summary, I learned more about modeling with Fusion 360 and improving designs over time by prototyping them frequently.

Although I have not gotten to test it, I plan to do so within the next year or so, as my interest in model rocketry is growing, and I'd like to test out something I made.
